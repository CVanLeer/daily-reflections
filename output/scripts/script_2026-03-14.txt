Good morning, Chris.

Right now it is forty four point nine degrees under a clear sky. Cool and crisp. You are heading toward a beautiful one today. High around seventy seven, low about forty two point seven, and the forecast says zero percent chance of rain. Sun all the way through.

Around the world, the Senate just passed its first big housing bill since the old subprime mess. They are saying it could ease regulations and pump more money into getting homes built, but it still has to survive the rest of Washington. On the foreign front, reports say former president Trump told other leaders that Iran is, quote, about to surrender. And inside the White House, word is the vice president has been one of the skeptical voices about striking Iran, pushing back behind closed doors.

But put all that aside for a second.  

Stop.  

Breathe.

Just you and the quiet for a moment.  

Let the noise of the headlines sit outside the room. Let the to do list wait at the door.

“Be still, and know that I am God.” That is the invitation from the Psalms. Not “hurry and fix everything.” Be still.

Paul told the Philippians, “I have learned the secret of being content in any and every situation.” Learned. Which means it was trained, not gifted.

C. S. Lewis said, “Relying on God has to begin all over again every day as if nothing had yet been done.” Fresh start. Every morning.

And you know how coaches talk. One of them said, “You do not rise to the occasion, you fall to the level of your training.” Body. Mind. Spirit. All of it.

So this morning, just ask, quietly, “Lord, show me how to walk with you in the imperfect. In the interruptions. In the so called bad days.”  

Let that be enough for right now.

All right. Let us talk about joy in the rain… on a day with no rain.

Because here is the funny thing. You have got a clear sky, sunshine on the way, almost no chance of anything messing up the weather. And your brain will still find something that feels “off.”

Maybe you slept weird. Maybe you feel a little flat, a little foggy. Maybe something from yesterday is still hanging over you. Maybe the house is a mess, the schedule is cluttered, or there is that one relationship that feels like a pebble in your shoe.

So even on a perfect weather Saturday, there is always some kind of rain.

The line for today is this. “If you do not learn to find joy in the rain you will have far less joy in your life and precisely the same amount of rain.”

That is brutal. And it is true.

Your life is going to have the same number of flat days. The same number of interrupted plans. The same number of “this is not how I pictured it” mornings. The only variable is how you meet them.

Your brain loves to bargain. It says, “I will start taking care of myself when work calms down.” “I will really enjoy my mornings when I am in the next house.” “I will relax when this one problem is solved.” It keeps moving the starting line.

It is like standing at the window, waiting for a clear forecast before you decide whether you are allowed to be okay.

But life is not going to clear the runway for you. There will always be some kind of drizzle. Some kind of wind. Some kind of delay.

So the question becomes, what do you train for?

Do you train for the perfect day? Or do you train for the day you actually get.

Here is where the body quietly runs the show.

You know how it goes. You wake up off. Shoulders tight. Thoughts already spinning a little. The temptation is to match your behavior to your mood. Stay slumped. Scroll. Skip breakfast. Tell yourself, “It is just one of those days.” And once your body adopts that posture, your mind follows it down.

The flip side is just as real. You can be in the exact same circumstances, same unfinished projects, same unsolved problems… but you decide to move your body in a different direction. And the whole day bends with it.

You step outside into the chill of forty four degrees for five minutes. You feel it on your face. You let your lungs work a little. You come back in and stretch your back and shoulders for two minutes instead of reaching for your phone. You splash cold water on your face and neck. You eat something with protein instead of just sugar and caffeine.

Nothing in the outside world has changed. Same news. Same work. Same responsibilities. But the instrument you are going to play the day with, your body, is tuned differently. And a tuned instrument makes the same song feel completely different.

We underestimate how physical joy is.

We talk about joy like it is this mystical emotion that drops out of the sky when the conditions are right. But so much of it is embodied. It is breath. Posture. Blood flow. Light on your eyes. Muscles doing what they were built to do.

Even your thoughts ride on chemistry. And chemistry responds to simple, small inputs. A walk. A glass of water. Some sunlight. A real meal.

So when we say, “learn to find joy in the rain,” part of that is very practical. It is saying, “Even on a gray, sluggish, imperfect morning, I still have levers I can pull.”

And the deeper layer is this. You are not just pulling those levers to feel a little better. You are training for a different kind of life.

There is a philosophy question hiding under your Saturday. What do you believe a good life actually looks like?

If your picture of a good life is, “Everything goes my way, my schedule is smooth, my energy is high, my body feels perfect, and people behave how I want,” then you will almost never get to be happy. Because reality will keep failing your fantasy.

But if your picture of a good life is, “I walk faithfully with God in a world that is never fully under my control. I learn to respond well to what is given, not just what I picked,” then every messy, half broken, slightly inconvenient day becomes training ground.

You do not wait for the storm to pass. You learn how to walk in the storm. Sometimes you even learn to dance in it.

Joy then is not denial. It is not pretending the rain is sunshine. It is choosing to say, “The rain is real. The frustration is real. The fatigue is real. And I am still going to look for what is good. I am still going to move my body in a way that honors the gift. I am still going to treat this day as usable.”

Think about it like this. When you lift weights, the weight is the resistance. You do not wait for the bar to be lighter before you call it a workout. The whole point is that it is heavy.

In the same way, the imperfect conditions of your day are the weight. The canceled plans. The low energy. The weird mood. That is the bar on the rack. The workout is how you respond.

You can say, “Not today, this feels too heavy,” and walk out of the gym. Or you can say, “Okay. Maybe I do not hit a personal record today. But I can still practice good form. I can still show up.”

So here is your little experiment for this Saturday.

Find one small, slightly uncomfortable physical thing to do today in less than ideal conditions. Not to punish yourself. To practice.

Maybe it is stepping out into the morning chill for a ten minute walk before the day really starts, even if you would rather stay under the blanket. Maybe it is a short body weight circuit on the living room floor when you feel like collapsing on the couch. Maybe it is turning the shower a little cooler for thirty seconds at the end, just to remind yourself that you can breathe through discomfort.

Do not wait for your mood to line up first. Let the action lead, and let the mood follow.

And as you do it, do not grit your teeth and white knuckle your way through. Look for the joy in it.

The joy of knowing, “I am the kind of man who can move even when I do not feel like it.” The joy of feeling your heart beat a little faster. The joy of stepping back inside from the cold and feeling the warm air hit your skin. The joy of keeping a promise to yourself.

That is how you train your nervous system to stop panicking every time life is not ideal. You are teaching your body and mind, “We can be okay here. We can even be grateful here.”

Over time, that changes the story in your head. Instead of, “I can only enjoy life when everything lines up,” it becomes, “I can find something good, even here. I can move toward God, even here. I can take care of this body, even here.”

You do that enough Saturdays in a row, and suddenly you are not at the mercy of the weather, or the news, or the mood that greets you when you open your eyes. You are still affected by them, of course. You are human. But you are not owned by them.

The rain keeps coming. But it stops deciding who you are.

So, as the sun comes up on this clear, cool morning, do not wait for a different day to start living well. Use this one. Exactly as it is.

Now, go get after it.
