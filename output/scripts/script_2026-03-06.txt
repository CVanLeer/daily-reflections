Good morning, Chris.

We are rolling into this Friday together. Weather’s a little mysterious today, forecasts have been fuzzy, so plan for layers… light jacket this morning, something you can peel off if the sun decides to show up later. Keep an eye on the sky and on that weather app, because it could go either way.

Out in the world, things are heavy. Iran has launched new attacks, promising that the United States will, quote, bitterly regret, unquote, the sinking of that warship, and calling for the former president’s blood. Missiles are still flying toward Israel as that conflict grinds into a sixth day. The United States is saying it is just getting started with strikes in Iran, pushing the fight farther out into the region. And over in Europe, Spain’s prime minister is openly rebuking this war, standing off against Washington and refusing to bend to trade threats. A lot of noise, a lot of pressure, a lot of people moving pieces on the board.

But put all that aside for a second.  

Stop.  

Breathe.

Right here, before your phone, before your calendar, before your to‑do list, there is this moment. Just you and God and the day He has handed you.

“Be still, and know that I am God.” That is from the Psalms. Not “do more and know,” not “prove yourself and know.” Be still.

Paul told the church in Corinth that each person is like a different part of a body. The eye cannot say to the hand, “I do not need you.” In other words, no part is extra. No part is decoration.

C. S. Lewis once wrote that there are no ordinary people. That the person you brush past at the office, or in the grocery store, is an eternal being. No ordinary people. That includes you.

And you know how a good coach talks to his team. “If you bust an assignment, somebody else has to cover your gap.” Your job is not small, because the gap you cover is not small.

So just sit with this for a breath.  

You are not background noise in your own life.  

Your presence matters.

Listen, here is what I want to talk to you about this morning. There is a quiet belief that smart, introspective people carry around. It sounds something like this: “If I disappeared from this meeting, this project, this house, people would adjust. They would be fine. Someone else could do what I do.”

And when that belief settles in, even a little, it starts to shape how you move.

You show up… but not fully.  
You are on the call… but half in your head.  
You are in the room at home… but scrolling, or replaying the day, or gaming out the next move.

You start coasting. Not because you are lazy. But because you have convinced yourself you are… optional. Replaceable. A nice‑to‑have.

Part of that is just how the human mind is wired. You have a built‑in negativity filter. You will remember the one awkward comment you made in a meeting for three days. You will forget the three calm, clear, strategic things you said that helped everyone else get aligned.

You will replay the one moment you stumbled as a dad, a husband, a friend. The sharp tone. The distracted answer. The time you were late. But you will not replay the hundred quiet moments where you were steady, where you listened, where you carried weight without complaint.

Your brain exaggerates your misses and minimizes your hits. That is the filter.

Then you add that other voice. The one that whispers, “If they really knew how often you are guessing… If they really knew how much you do not feel ready… they would not trust you so much.” That is that imposter feeling. It tells you that the trust others place in you is a misunderstanding, a glitch in the system, and sooner or later, the universe will correct the error.

So you discount your contributions. You explain them away.  
“They would have figured it out without me.”  
“I just wrote it down, they did the real work.”  
“I am just doing my job.”  

Meanwhile, the people around you are quietly leaning on you like a pillar.

Think about your work like a system, not just a job. You have a particular way of thinking. A way you see patterns. A way you connect dots, or anticipate problems, or simplify chaos into steps.

When you do that consistently, you become a stabilizing force. People do not always say it out loud, but they start to plan around the assumption that you will be… you.

They assume you will be prepared.  
They assume you will have thought through the angles.  
They assume you will catch the risk they did not see, or ask the question no one else thought to ask.

And because they assume it, they stop covering that gap themselves. They free up their mental energy, their emotional energy, because you are carrying that slice of the load.

Now imagine you are not there. Or you are there, but not really. You checked out. You are tired, or resentful, or just convinced it does not matter if you bring your full self today.

What happens?  

Someone else has to carry weight that was meant for you.  
The meeting drifts.  
The decision gets made on shallow thinking.  
The risk goes unnoticed.  
The standard slips, just a little.

Most days, no one will walk up to you and say, “Hey, I really felt the hole you left today.” People are polite. They patch it up. They improvise. But the hole is real.

Same thing at home.

You might tell yourself, “If I miss bedtime tonight, it is not a big deal. If I am half on my phone while we talk, they will understand. If I do not initiate that hard conversation, we will get to it eventually.”

But in a house, in a marriage, in a friendship, your presence is part of the emotional structure. When you are engaged, when you ask the second question, when you listen all the way to the end, the other person’s nervous system settles. They feel held. They feel less alone.

When you are absent, someone else has to carry that emotional weight. Your wife has to be the encourager and the planner and the spiritual anchor. Your kids learn to self‑soothe and self‑explain in ways that age them up too fast. Your friends learn not to bring you the deeper things, because you always seem “busy in your head.”

They may not complain. They may not even consciously notice. But again, the hole is real.

Leadership is not a title. It is a choice to show up fully, because you understand that your absence is never neutral. It always shifts weight onto someone else.

Being on time, prepared, present, and honest… those are not just personal virtues. They are ways of saying to the people around you, “You can lean on me. This part, I have it.”

On time says, “You do not have to carry the anxiety of wondering if I will show.”  
Prepared says, “You do not have to burn energy re‑explaining the basics.”  
Present says, “You are not alone in this moment. I am here with you, not just near you.”  
Honest says, “You can trust the ground under your feet when you are with me.”

You may think, “Well, that is nice, but they would survive without me.” Of course they would. That is not the point.

The point is that God has given you a specific slice of the world to steward. A few people. A few problems. A few rooms where your mind and your voice and your steadiness are meant to be part of how He cares for others.

In that sense, your presence is not a luxury. It is an assignment.

And you, of all people, understand systems. You know that if one gear stops turning, the machine does not explode immediately. It just wears down faster. Friction increases. Little cracks appear.

So here is the invitation for today.

When you walk into that first meeting, or open that laptop, or step into the kitchen, do it with the quiet awareness that you are carrying a piece of the structure on your shoulders. Not all of it. Just your piece.

Ask yourself, “If I mentally step out right now, who has to carry what I was meant to carry?” Let that question pull you back into the moment.

And when that old belief shows up… “I am replaceable, they would be fine without me”… notice it, name it, and answer it with truth.

The truth is, yes, God can replace anyone. He is not limited. But He chose you, here, now, with these people, on this day. That means something.

Do not underestimate the hole your absence would leave. In the project. In the room. In the heart of the person sitting across from you.

So today, Chris, design your day like a system where your full presence is a non‑negotiable input. Protect your focus. Guard your energy. Say less, but mean every word. When you are there, be there.

You do not need to be loud. You do not need to be flashy. You just need to be faithful to the slice of weight that has your name on it.

And as you do, trust that God is using your steady, often quiet, contribution in ways you may never get to see.

Now, go get after it.

Quote of the day: “Do not underestimate the hole your absence would leave.”
