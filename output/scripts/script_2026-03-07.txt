Good morning, Chris.  

Clear skies over you this Saturday. Right now you are sitting at about sixty two degrees, heading up toward a warm high around seventy nine. It will dip back to the low sixties tonight. There is a decent chance of rain, a little under fifty percent, so it might tease you with a shower later on, but for now it is clean, open sky.

On the money front, Dow futures are ticking a bit higher after a rough ride. That index is still staring down its worst week since October, driven by that jump in oil prices. Overseas, Azerbaijan is asking Iran for an official explanation after a drone attack, they are still looking into the technical details there. And in the business world, the heir to EssilorLuxottica is working on a multibillion dollar deal to buy out his siblings’ stakes in the family holding company.  

But put all that aside for a second.  

Stop.  

Breathe.  

Let the shoulders come down. Feel where your feet are. You woke up. That is grace already.

“Do you not know that your body is a temple of the Holy Spirit within you? You are not your own, for you were bought with a price. So glorify God in your body.” That is Paul, reminding you this frame you walk around in is not cheap plastic. It is on loan.

C. S. Lewis said, “You do not have a soul. You are a soul. You have a body.” The soul is the driver, but the car still matters.

And I think about the old coaching line, “How you do anything is how you do everything.” The little choices with your body, your food, your sleep, your movement, they are not separate from your spiritual life. They are where it shows up.

So right here, this morning, just ask, “Lord, what do you want to train in me today?” Not, “What do I feel like?” But, “What are You building?”  

Because that is where we are going today.  

Listen, it is Saturday. The whole culture preaches the same sermon on a day like this.  

“You earned it.”  
“Treat yourself.”  
“Take it easy, you deserve to do nothing.”  

And there is a place for real rest. God Himself rested. But there is a difference between rest and escape. Between restoration and sedation.

A typical “treat yourself” Saturday looks something like this. Sleep in way past what your body actually needs, not because you are tired, but because you do not want to face the day. Scroll in bed. Coffee becomes sugar with caffeine in it. Breakfast is whatever is fast and salty. You tell yourself you will work out later, but the couch pulls you in. Maybe an extra drink at lunch because, hey, it is the weekend. Drive through for dinner. Then more screens till midnight and you call that “recharging.”

But if you zoom out, and you run that pattern not for one Saturday, but for fifty Saturdays in a row, what does it buy you? You get softer. Slower. More anxious. More restless. Sleep gets worse. Joints ache. Mood swings more. Spirit feels dull. You start to feel like life is heavier than it really is.

That is what I mean when I say comfort is a kind of addiction. Not the blanket itself. Not the couch. Not the burger. It is the pattern of always choosing the easiest, numbing option. The pattern of saying, “If it is uncomfortable, it must be bad.”  

That pattern will quietly steal your strength. And once it has your strength, it starts stealing your joy.

Anyone who has ever battled a serious habit knows this. It almost never starts at the bottom. It starts with “I just want to relax.” “I just want to take the edge off.” “I just want to not feel this for a little while.” The chase for ease and numbness always wants a little more. A little more food. A little more drink. A little more screen. A little more distraction.

And while that chase is escalating, the quiet disciplines are the first to go. Sleep gets sloppy. Movement disappears. Meals become whatever is closest. You stop listening to your body, and start obeying your cravings.

But here is the good news, Chris. You can flip that script.  

Your body can become the training ground where you practice choosing what is good over what is merely comfortable. Not in some huge dramatic way. In small, simple, repeatable ways that stack up.

Think about this morning. You could make today a lab. A controlled experiment in being the kind of man who is not ruled by comfort.

An early walk. Nothing fancy. Just lace up, step outside while the air is still cool, and move. You are not chasing a personal record. You are just saying to your body, “We move, even when the bed feels better.” That is not punishment. That is practice.

A cold shower. Not because cold water makes you a hero, but because it is a safe, small place to feel discomfort and stay calm. You step in, your body screams, “Nope, we hate this,” and you breathe. You relax your jaw. You let the water hit and you do not bolt. That is you proving to yourself, “I can feel something unpleasant and I do not have to run from it. I am not a slave to comfort.”

A simple workout. Push ups. Squats. A few sets with whatever you have. Enough to raise your heart rate and remind your muscles that they are made for work. Again, not as punishment for what you ate, but as rehearsal. “When this gets hard, I keep going. I do not quit at the first sign of burn.”

Cooking real food. Taking twenty or thirty minutes to make something with actual ingredients. Protein. Vegetables. Water. Sitting down at a table instead of eating in the car. That is you choosing fuel over fog. Long term strength over short term flavor blast.

None of these are glamorous. But that is the point. They are small, physical ways you practice a deeper truth: “I will not let my cravings run my life.”

People who have walked the road of addiction will tell you, the little disciplines around sleep, movement, and food are not side quests. They are core support beams for staying clear and steady. When you go to bed on time, move your body, and eat something that does not come in a crinkly bag, your mind is calmer. Your emotions are less frantic. You are less likely to reach for the old numbing tools.

You are building a life where you do not need to escape as often. Because your baseline is more stable.

So when you feel that pull today, that voice that says, “Come on, man, just skip the walk, just order the thing, just binge the show, you can start again Monday,” hear it for what it is. That is not rest talking. That is the addiction to comfort, asking for another hit.

You do not have to scream at it. You do not have to hate yourself for hearing it. You just calmly answer, “Not today. Today we train.”  

And here is the key shift. Stop seeing discomfort as punishment. Start seeing it as practice.

When you hold a plank and your core is shaking, that is not your body failing you. That is your body adapting to the signal you are sending. When you say no to the extra drink, or the late night junk, you are not depriving yourself. You are investing in a stronger, clearer version of you that wakes up tomorrow with more power, not less.

Every time you choose a walk over the couch, you are casting a vote for the man you want to be five years from now. Every time you choose water over the sugary thing, sleep over another episode, real food over drive through, you are saying, “I can be trusted with more. With more responsibility. With more people depending on me. With bigger assignments from God.”

Because if you can be faithful with your own body, you can be faithful with a team. A family. A calling.  

This is why the ancient writers talk so much about self control. Not because God is obsessed with you being miserable. But because a man who can master his appetites is free. You cannot lead if you are always negotiating with your cravings.

So make this Saturday a little different. Not a boot camp. Not a punishment day. A lab. A place where you experiment with being the man who does not bow to comfort.

Maybe for you that means:  
“I will get outside for twenty minutes, no phone.”  
“I will take a colder shower than I want, and breathe through it.”  
“I will cook one real meal.”  
“I will be in bed at a time that serves tomorrow, not just tonight.”  

String those together, and you will end this day tired in the right way. Not drained and numb, but used. Spent on purpose.  

And when your head hits the pillow, you will know, “I did not let the couch, or the cravings, or the sugar, or the screen write my story today. I did.”  

That feeling, Chris, is worth more than any quick hit of comfort. That is what it feels like to be alive on purpose.

Now, go get after it.  

“Comfort is the worst addiction.” — attributed to Marcus Aurelius.
