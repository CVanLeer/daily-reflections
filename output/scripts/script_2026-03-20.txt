Good morning, Chris.

You are up with The Voice. It is Friday, March twentieth, and we are rolling into the end of the week. Weather’s a bit of a mystery this morning, forecasts have been fuzzy, so plan for layers and a jacket on standby, just in case the day cannot make up its mind between cool and mild.

Out in the world, money people are watching the Federal Reserve hold interest rates steady, with a hint of just one possible cut later in the year. Over on the geopolitical front, the tension dial is high. You have threats flying about Iranian gas fields and talk of what happens if Iran goes after infrastructure in Qatar. And Japan’s prime minister is meeting with President Trump, trying to secure help for safe passage through the Strait of Hormuz while a war in Iran reshapes everybody’s travel plans and priorities.

But put all that aside for a second.  

Stop.  

Breathe.

Let the headlines move to the edges of the room. You are here. You are breathing. God has already been in this day before you woke up.

“Be still, and know that I am God.” That is Psalm forty six. Not, “figure it all out,” not, “feel perfectly calm,” just, “be still, and know.”

Paul told the Philippians, “Let your gentleness be evident to all. The Lord is near.” That is a line about emotional steadiness. About being the same person when things are smooth and when things are rough.

C. S. Lewis wrote that feelings come and go, but the love of God does not. And old school coaches will tell their players, “Play the game, not your emotions.” Same truth, different language.

For a moment, just breathe in slowly… and breathe out even slower.  

“Lord, order my inner world today. Let truth lead, not my moods.”

Alright. Let us talk about that inner world.

I want you to imagine, just for a second, that inside your chest there is a boardroom. Long table. Big chairs. And all your emotions have nameplates.

Fear is there. He is early. He always is. He has a stack of risk reports and worst case scenarios. He is not evil. He is just loud.

Anger is there. Arms crossed. Tapping a pen. He hates inefficiency and injustice. He is ready to flip the table if someone wastes time or disrespects you.

Excitement bursts into the room late, coffee in hand, tossing out wild ideas. “Let us launch this. Let us buy that. Let us say yes to everything.”

Envy is in the corner. Quiet. Watching who gets what. Who is ahead. Who is behind. He keeps whispering, “You are falling behind. You should have more by now.”

Now picture this. The company is called “Your Life Incorporated.” And any one of those voices can try to grab the chief executive chair.

When fear runs the company, everything becomes about safety. You do not take the call. You do not ask the question. You do not send the email. The company survives, maybe, but it never grows. It just hides.

When anger runs the company, people walk on eggshells. You might get quick results in the short term, but you burn bridges, scorch trust, and your best people quietly start looking for the exit.

When excitement runs the company, it feels fun. Until you look at the balance sheet. Commitments everywhere. No focus. Projects half started. Energy sprayed in every direction.

When envy runs the company, nothing is ever enough. Wins feel small. You cannot celebrate your own progress because you are staring at someone else’s quarterly report.

You know this. You have seen leaders like that. You have been that guy on some days.

The truth is, none of those emotions are the enemy. They are not supposed to be fired. They are supposed to be consultants.

Fear says, “Hey, there is real risk here. Slow down. Double check the numbers.” That is useful.

Anger says, “Something unjust just happened. A boundary was crossed. A value was violated.” That is useful.

Excitement says, “There is opportunity here. There is energy here. This could be good.” That is useful.

Envy, even envy, says, “You care about growth. You care about your potential. You see something in their life that mirrors a desire in yours.” That can be useful.

The problem is not that they speak. The problem is when you let them vote, and then you hand them the keys to the whole company.

Clear thinking, the part of you that is grounded in God, in your values, in your long term vision, sits at the head of the table. That is the chief executive. That is the part of you that remembers, “I am here to be faithful, not frantic. I am here to live by principle, not by impulse.”

So your job today is not to silence your emotions. Your job is to chair the meeting.

A feeling walks in. You say, “Fear, thanks for coming. What are you seeing that I might be missing?” You listen. You write it down. And then you ask, “Given my values, given my commitments, given my long game, what is the right move anyway?”

That is very different from, “I feel scared, so I will not act.”

Same with anger. Something happens at work. Someone drops the ball, or speaks to you in a way that is not okay. Anger jumps up, ready to fire off the email, ready to slam the door.

You, as chair, say, “Alright, anger, what are you trying to protect?” Maybe it is respect. Maybe it is fairness. Maybe it is excellence. Then you let your wiser self decide, “How do I protect that value without burning the place down?”

You move from reaction to response.

This is not just about you, by the way. When you lead anything, a team, a family, a project, your emotional life becomes part of their weather.

If you are stormy, they spend their energy reading the clouds, not doing their work. If they are wondering, “What version of him is walking in the door today?” they are managing you instead of managing their responsibilities.

But when you are steady, not flat, not robotic, just steady, they relax. They can bring you bad news without fear. They can tell you the truth. They can think clearly because you are not another variable. You are the constant.

And that steadiness does not come from pretending you do not feel. It comes from feeling deeply, and then choosing wisely.

So let us make this practical for today.

At some point, maybe in the next few hours, a strong feeling is going to hit you. Could be frustration in traffic. Could be anxiety before a call. Could be a spike of defensiveness when someone gives you feedback.

When that wave hits, here is the move.

First, pause. Even two seconds. A breath in, a breath out. That tiny gap is where your freedom lives.

Second, name it. “This is fear.” “This is anger.” “This is embarrassment.” Naming it moves it from being the water you are drowning in, to something you are looking at.

Third, ask, “What are you trying to tell me?” Maybe fear is saying, “You care about this outcome.” Maybe anger is saying, “You feel disrespected.” Maybe sadness is saying, “You lost something that mattered.”

Fourth, and this is the key, ask, “What would the calm, long term version of me decide here?” Not the scared version. Not the insulted version. The version of you who is five years wiser, closer to God, more aligned with who you want to be.

And then, as best you can, follow that answer, not the first impulse.

This is how you build an inner structure. You are training your mind to treat emotions as data, not dictators. You are saying, “You all get a voice, but you do not get the vote.”

Over time, something beautiful happens. Those same emotions start to trust the process. Fear does not have to scream, because it knows you will listen. Anger does not have to explode, because it knows you will protect what matters. Excitement does not have to hijack the wheel, because it knows you will make room for joy and adventure in the right places.

And through it all, you are anchored in something bigger than your moods. In a God who does not change with the market, or the news cycle, or the way you feel on a Friday morning.

So as you step into this day, you are not just a passenger in your own head. You are the one running the meeting.

Listen well. Decide slowly. Act in line with who you are called to be, not just how you feel in the moment.

Now, go get after it.

Quote of the day: “Emotions are incredible consultants, but terrible CEOs.” — Anonymous.
