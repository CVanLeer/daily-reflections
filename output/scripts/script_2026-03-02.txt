Good morning, Chris.

It is Monday, March second, twenty twenty six. Let us call it a cool, gray start in your world today. Think mid forties rolling toward the mid fifties by afternoon. A little cloud cover hanging around, maybe a few breaks of sun later. Good weather to keep your head down, get some real work done, and not feel bad about being indoors.

In the news this morning, a heavy one. Reports say Iran’s supreme leader, Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, has been killed in a joint strike by the United States and Israel. Protests are already flaring. Hundreds of people stormed the United States consulate in Pakistan overnight as anger spills into the streets. And closer to home, the Washington Post is running a column from a grandfather wrestling with a familiar question. His grandson has no motivation, and he feels like his daughter is enabling him. That tension between loving someone and holding a line.

Alright. That is the world outside.

But put all that aside for a second. Stop. Breathe.

In through your nose… slow… hold… and out through your mouth. Let the shoulders drop. Let the jaw unclench. You are here. It is early. It is just you and God and the quiet before the noise.

“Teach us to number our days, that we may gain a heart of wisdom.” That is the psalm. Not, “Teach us to fantasize about our years.” Just today. This one.

Paul told the Corinthians, “Let all that you do be done in love.” Not all that you plan. All that you do. The actions.

C. S. Lewis said, “The smallest good act today is the capture of a strategic point from which, a few months later, you may be able to go on to victories you never dreamed of.” Tiny moves. Strategic points.

And you know how coaches talk in the locker room. “Do not worry about the championship. Win this play. Do your job.” Same idea. Different language.

So this morning, Chris, just ask, “What is my job in the next ninety minutes?” Not the whole week. The next ninety.

Let us talk about that.

Here is the thing about Mondays. There is this fantasy that shows up with the alarm. “New week, new me.” You know the script. This is the week I eat clean, inbox zero, crush the gym, lead like a warrior monk, and somehow also be the most present husband, father, friend.

And then it is ten a.m.

You have two surprise meetings. Someone pinged you with a “quick question” that turned into thirty minutes. You are already behind. And you realize… you are not living a new week. You are just running last week’s code. The habits you trained, the defaults you never changed, the systems you did or did not build. That is what is driving the day.

You do not rise to the level of your goals. You fall to the level of your systems.

Goals are like the inspirational poster on the wall of the gym. Systems are the shoes that are either sitting in the closet… or already laced on your feet.

So, on a Monday morning like this, with the world feeling volatile and loud, there is a quiet kind of power in narrowing your focus. Not “How do I dominate the week.” Just, “What can I actually control this morning.”

You can control your calendar more than you think. You can control your environment more than you think. You can absolutely control your first ninety minutes.

You cannot control if someone drops a crisis on your desk at three p.m. You cannot control the news cycle. You cannot control what your coworkers choose to do. But you can decide, “For the first ninety minutes, this is what happens. Every Monday. No negotiation.”

Think of it like laying rails. Once the rails are down, the train just runs. You do not need to white knuckle the steering wheel every Monday. You just need to build the tracks once, and then protect them.

So, let us make this very small and very practical.

Imagine you had only two Monday systems. Not ten. Just two. Tiny, repeatable, boring in the best way.

First, a morning planning ritual.

Not a thirty page journal. Not a life review. I am talking ten to fifteen minutes, same time, same place, same steps.

Maybe it looks like this. You sit down with a coffee. You open your calendar and your task list. You ask three questions.

One. What are the one to three things that, if done by end of day, would make today a win.

Two. When exactly will I do them. You block the time. You make appointments with yourself like you would with anyone else.

Three. What can I drop, delegate, or delay so those blocks are real, not fiction.

You write those one to three things on a card, a sticky note, something physical you can see. That is it. That is the ritual. Same steps, every Monday. No drama. No debate.

The power is not in how fancy it is. The power is in the fact that it is automatic. You roll out of bed, and Monday has rails. You do the ritual because “this is what I do on Mondays,” not because you feel especially inspired.

Second, a shutdown routine.

Most people let Monday bleed into the night. Laptop open, mind spinning, phone buzzing. And then they wonder why Tuesday feels like a hangover.

What if you had a fifteen minute shutdown that happens at the same time every Monday. Again, simple.

You review. What got done. What did not. You move any unfinished tasks to tomorrow on purpose, not by forgetting them. You write down the first thing you will do when you sit down on Tuesday. Then, you say, “Today is closed.” You close the laptop. You walk away.

It is a line in the sand for your brain. Work is now off. Life is now on. That is a system. It is not sexy. But it is a gift to your future self.

And here is another one that matters more than people think. A rule for interruptions.

Mondays are ambush day. People want your attention, your time, your energy. If you do not have a rule, you will live in other people’s priorities.

A rule can be as simple as, “During my deep work block, I do not respond to messages. After the block, I batch responses for ten minutes.” Or, “If someone interrupts, I ask, ‘Is this urgent or can we schedule ten minutes later.’”

You decide your version. Then you stick to it. Calmly. Respectfully. You are not being difficult. You are being a good steward of what you have been given.

All of these are just ways of saying, “I will not rely on willpower. I will rely on design.”

And this is where leadership comes in.

Leadership is not standing on a chair and giving a speech about productivity. It is going first with structure.

When you quietly build better systems for yourself, you raise the floor for everyone around you. People feel it.

They see that your Mondays are less frantic. They notice that you are present in meetings because you are not constantly firefighting. They watch you shut down at a sane hour and still deliver. You do not have to preach. Your systems preach for you.

At home, it is the same. If you have a predictable shutdown, if you have a clear moment where work ends and family begins, the people you love feel that. They feel that you are not half in and half out. That stability becomes a kind of shelter for them. Especially in a world where headlines are heavy and the ground feels like it is shifting.

And under all of this is something deeper.

You are not building systems so you can be a machine. You are building systems so you can be faithful.

Faithful with your time. Faithful with your gifts. Faithful with the people entrusted to you.

You set rails on Monday so that you are free to respond to the unexpected without your whole life derailing. When a real crisis hits, or someone truly needs you, you can step in from a place of order, not chaos.

So, as you step into this Monday, do not try to rewrite your whole life by noon. Just pick one small rail you are going to lay today.

Maybe it is that ten minute planning ritual, and you actually block time for it on your calendar. Maybe it is deciding your shutdown time and telling someone, “At five forty five, I close the laptop.” Maybe it is writing down your rule for interruptions and trying it for one day.

One rail. One system. Built on purpose.

You do that this Monday. And next Monday. And the one after that. Over time, you will look up and realize you did not rise to the level of your goals. You quietly raised the level of your systems. And that raised the level of your life.

Now, go get after it.
