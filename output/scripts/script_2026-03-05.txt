Good morning, Chris.  
Fifty five degrees under a clear sky right now, just a beautiful, crisp start. You are heading for a warm afternoon, high around seventy eight, with the low tonight right back down near fifty five. Almost no chance of rain, about two percent, so it is a good day to be out moving around.

Around the world, a few things on the board. In Texas politics, James Talarico is projected to win the Democratic primary in that state Senate race. On the money side, Goldman Sachs chief David Solomon is saying markets have been surprisingly calm so far in response to the conflict in the Middle East, and that it may take weeks before we really understand the impact. And in Washington, House investigators are widening their look into Jeffrey Epstein’s network, now asking people like Bill Gates and former White House lawyer Kathryn Ruemmler for interviews.

But put all that aside for a second.  
Stop.  
Breathe.

Let your shoulders drop.  
Unclench your jaw.  
Take one slow breath in through your nose.  
Hold it.  
And let it go.

“Give us this day our daily bread.” Not this week. Not this year. This day.

Paul told the church in Corinth, “We walk by faith, not by sight.” Not by spreadsheets, not by worst case scenarios, not by the noise in our heads. By faith.

C. S. Lewis said, “Relying on God has to begin all over again every day as if nothing had yet been done.” Fresh start. Every sunrise.

And you know how coaches talk. “Do your job. One play at a time.” Not the whole season. Just this snap.

So, this morning… you and I are going to sit for a minute with that. One day. One step. One honest action.

Because here is the thing about Thursdays.  
By now, the week has some weight to it.

Monday you are charged up. Tuesday you are in the groove. Wednesday you are grinding. By Thursday, you are tired, the inbox is full, the texts you have not answered are staring at you, and the little problems from Monday have grown legs.

And your mind wants to do what it always does when it is tired.  
It starts spinning.

“What if this deal falls through. What if that conversation blows up. What if I never get on top of this debt. What if I am always this behind. What if I am not enough for what is coming.”

You know that feeling. You are sitting at your desk or in your car, and your body is in Thursday morning, but your brain is already living in ten imaginary disasters that have not happened yet.

There is a simple, hard practice that was born for days like this.  
Live one day at a time. Do the next right thing.

Not, “Fix your whole life in your head before nine in the morning.”  
Not, “Solve every relationship, every bill, every health issue, every piece of your past.”  

Just, “What is the next right thing I can actually do. Right now. In this twenty four hours.”

Sometimes that next right thing is tiny.  
Make the call you have been avoiding.  
Send the honest email instead of the vague one.  
Apologize for the sharp word from yesterday.  
Put the phone face down and actually listen to the person in front of you.  
Get up and take a ten minute walk instead of doom scrolling.  
Pray. Out loud if you have to. “God, I do not know what to do, but I am here.”

When people hit bottom and start rebuilding their lives, they learn this the hard way. You cannot think your way out of the pit. You cannot worry your way into freedom. You cannot white knuckle your way into peace.

You learn to live in slices.  
This hour.  
This conversation.  
This decision.

“Do the next right thing” sounds almost too simple. Your brain will say, “Come on, that is not strategic enough. I need a master plan. I need to know how this all turns out.”

But notice something.  
When you sit and obsess over problems, do they get smaller or bigger in your head?  
Do you feel more powerful or less?  
More clear or more foggy?

There is a line in that old book of wisdom from people who have walked through fire. “Live in the answer, not the problem, and the problem will go away.” That does not mean the situation magically vanishes. It means when you start acting on the small, clear answers you already have, the problem stops owning your mind.

Here is where faith comes in.  
God does not usually hand you the five year blueprint.  
He hands you the next step.

“Love your neighbor.” That is a step.  
“Tell the truth.” That is a step.  
“Do your work as if you are doing it for Me.” That is a step.  
“Come to Me, all who are weary and burdened, and I will give you rest.” That is a step. Turn toward Him instead of into the spin.

Grace has this strange habit. It tends to show up in motion.  
You pick up the phone to make the hard call, and suddenly the words come.  
You walk into the meeting you were dreading, and you find a bit more patience than you thought you had.  
You kneel beside your bed and stumble through a clumsy prayer, and somehow your chest loosens a little.

You did not think your way into that. You stepped into it.

Now, that does not mean you ignore reality.  
There are things today you cannot change.  
You cannot change what you said last week.  
You cannot change the global economy.  
You cannot change how someone else chooses to respond to you.

You can acknowledge that without surrendering your power.  
“Okay, that is outside my hands today.”  
Then you ask, “What is inside my hands.”

Maybe what is in your hands is to own your part and make amends.  
Maybe it is to keep a boundary you know you need.  
Maybe it is to drink water, eat real food, and go to bed on time, so tomorrow’s version of you is not dragging chains.

Courage, today, might not look like some big dramatic move.  
It might look like sending the résumé instead of just thinking about it.  
It might look like putting your honest numbers on paper instead of avoiding your bank account.  
It might look like texting a friend, “I am not okay today, can we talk,” instead of trying to muscle through alone.

And a calm mind… that is a choice too.  
You cannot always stop the first fearful thought from showing up.  
But you do not have to invite it in, sit it on the couch, and make it dinner.

When your mind starts rehearsing disasters, you can say, “Not today.”  
“Today, I will deal with what is actually in front of me.”  
“I will not live in ten fake futures when God has only asked me to be faithful in this one real present.”

So, Chris, let us bring it all the way down to your Thursday.

You have some things hanging over you.  
Name them. In your head right now. Three of them.

Now ask, for each one, “What is the next right thing I can do about this today. Not the whole thing. Just one honest step.”

If there is no step you can take today, then your work is different.  
Your work is to accept that, hand it to God, and stop letting it chew on you.  
“Lord, this one is yours for today. Show me if there is something I am missing. Until then, I am going to do the things you have clearly put in front of me.”

You are not carrying this day alone.  
You are not expected to fix everything by sundown.  
You are asked to walk through this twenty four hours with integrity. With honesty. With a steady heart.

Just for today, you can do that.  
You can tell the truth.  
You can keep your word.  
You can treat your body like it matters.  
You can talk to God instead of just talking to yourself.  
You can lay your head down tonight knowing, “I did the next right thing, as best I could see it.”

Tomorrow will have its own weight. Its own grace.  
You do not have to borrow either one.

Now, go get after it.
