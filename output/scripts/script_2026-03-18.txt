Good morning, Chris.

It is a cold, clear Wednesday out there. Sky is wide open, stars fading out. You are sitting at about thirty one degrees right now. You will warm up to around fifty six this afternoon, then drop back to just above thirty tonight. Almost no chance of rain. Just crisp air and bright light.

In the world, it is heavy. Israel is saying an overnight strike killed Ali Larijani, a top Iranian security official, along with the head of the Basij militia. Over in Cuba, after that United States oil squeeze, the national power grid has collapsed. The whole island is in a blackout, while politicians here talk about having the “honor” of taking the place. And in Kabul, a hospital has been hit. Hundreds feared dead. The Taliban is pointing the finger at Pakistan, and that border is getting more dangerous by the day.

But put all that aside for a second.

Stop.

Breathe.

In through your nose… slow… hold… and out through your mouth.

Again.

Let the shoulders drop a little. Let the jaw unclench. You are here. You are alive. And God has not lost track of you.

“Be still, and know that I am God.” That is the psalm. Not “do more.” Not “fix everything.” Be still.

Paul told the Romans, “There is now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus.” That is either true, or it is not. And if it is true, then the voice in your head that never stops condemning you is not speaking for Him.

C. S. Lewis said, “Humility is not thinking less of yourself, but thinking of yourself less.” That is a different posture than self hatred. Less staring in the mirror, more looking up.

And you know how a good coach talks in the locker room after a bad game. Not, “You are garbage.” More like, “We own the mistakes. We correct the tape. Then we move on.” Truth without humiliation.

So let us talk about that voice in your head this morning.

“No one has been crueler than I have been to me.”

Whoever first said that, a lot of people could have signed their name under it. You probably could too.

You know that voice. The one that wakes up before you do. The one that is already talking when your feet hit the floor. You forget something small, and it says, “Of course you did, you idiot.” You lose your temper for ten seconds, and it says, “See, nothing has changed. You are exactly who you have always been.” You fall short of your own standard, and it does not just point out the miss. It attacks your whole identity.

Here is the strange thing. You would not talk to a stranger that way. You would not talk to a friend that way. You would not even talk to an enemy that way, not out loud. But somehow, it feels normal to turn that level of cruelty inward.

Where does that come from.

A lot of times, it starts as a strategy. Somewhere along the line, you learned, “If I am hard enough on myself, I will not slip. If I beat myself up, I will stay sharp.” Maybe a parent or a coach or a boss used shame as a tool, and you internalized it. Maybe you decided, “If I punish myself first, no one else can hurt me worse.”

It can feel like responsibility. Like maturity. “I do not make excuses. I hold myself accountable.”

But listen. There is a difference between accountability and abuse.

Accountability says, “I did this. It was wrong. I own it. I will repair what I can. I will change the pattern.” Abuse says, “I did this, which proves I am worthless. So I deserve to suffer. Over and over.”

One leads to growth. The other leads to a kind of private prison.

That harsh narrator in your head, the one that replays the same failures, is not actually interested in your transformation. It is interested in control. If it can keep you in shame, it can keep you from risk. If it can keep you believing you are fundamentally broken, it never has to let you try to live like you are actually redeemed.

And here is the twist. That inner cruelty can feel holy. It can feel like you are agreeing with God about your sin. But God convicts to heal. That voice convicts to wound.

Think about how God actually deals with people in scripture. David commits adultery and murder. When he finally faces it, he does not write, “I am trash and should never exist.” He writes, “Create in me a clean heart, O God, and renew a right spirit within me.” He names the sin clearly. Then he asks for a new heart, not a deeper pit.

That is the pattern. Honest inventory, then mercy. Confession, then cleansing. Not confession as an endless loop of self torture.

If you have ever sat in a circle of people who are trying to rebuild their lives, you see this. The ones who grow are not the ones who minimize their past. They tell the truth. Sometimes in brutal detail. But they do it with a strange gentleness toward themselves. “This is what I did. This is who I hurt. Here is how I am making it right. And I am not going to live chained to that moment forever.”

They do not keep re sentencing themselves for the same crime. They do not keep dragging the same case back into court, after the judge has already ruled.

That is what you are invited into.

So what does that look like for you today.

First, you tell the truth. Not about who you are in some vague, condemning way. About what you actually did or did not do. “I lost my temper with my kids.” “I checked out instead of leaning into that hard conversation.” “I broke a promise to myself.” Concrete. Specific.

Second, you ask, “What is mine to repair.” Maybe it is an apology. Maybe it is changing a system so that failure is less likely next time. Maybe it is just admitting to God, “I chose comfort over courage and I do not want to keep doing that.”

And then, and this is the part your inner critic hates, you stop the beating. You refuse to keep hitting replay on the same shame tape. You say, “I have looked at this. I have owned it. I am making amends. I will not keep calling myself names over it.”

You are not dodging responsibility. You are refusing self cruelty.

Here is a small experiment for today.

At some point, that voice is going to flare up. Maybe in traffic. Maybe in a meeting. Maybe when you remember something from years ago and your stomach flips.

When you feel it start, pause. Even just for one breath. And ask yourself, “If someone I loved did this, how would I talk to them.”

Not, “How would I let them off the hook.” How would you actually speak to them. You would probably say something like, “Yeah, you messed that up. Own it. Go make it right. But this does not define you. Get up.”

Then, for that moment, borrow that tone for yourself.

You are not lying to yourself. You are just refusing to be your own bully.

Over time, if you keep doing that, the internal script starts to change. The voice in your head starts to sound less like a furious judge, and more like a firm but kind coach. Still honest. Still demanding. But on your side.

And underneath all of that, there is a deeper voice. The one that said, “You are mine.” The one that went to a cross so that your worst day would not have the final word. That voice is not soft on sin. But it is relentless in mercy.

You can agree with that voice today. Or you can agree with the accuser. One leads to life. The other leads to endless self punishment.

You get to choose which one you rehearse.

So as you step into this clear, cold Wednesday, try this. Take responsibility. Do the inventory. Make the amends. Then, when that old narrator tries to drag you back into the cell, just say, “We are not doing that today.” And walk out into the light.

Now, go get after it.
