Good morning, Chris.

It is an overcast Monday where you are. Sitting at about sixty two point seven degrees right now. Gray ceiling overhead. The forecast says it will creep up to around sixty five for the high, then drop hard tonight down near thirty one. Big chance of rain today, about eighty six percent, so it is a “keep the jacket handy” kind of morning.

In the news, a heavy one to start the week. The Pentagon has identified six service members killed when a United States refueling plane went down in Iraq, including an air force captain from Covington. Over in sports, Italy kept its unlikely run alive in the World Baseball Classic, knocking out Puerto Rico eight to six to move into the semifinals. And on the global stage, the former president is calling for the world to help open the Strait of Hormuz, even as the United States embassy in Baghdad was hit amid ongoing tensions.

But put all that aside for a second.

Stop.

Breathe.

Let your shoulders drop. Unclench your jaw. Feel your feet on the floor, or your hands on the steering wheel. You are here. It is Monday. And God has already seen the end of this week.

“Cast all your cares on Him, because He cares for you.” That is Peter. Simple. Direct. You are not meant to drag this week alone.

Paul told the Romans, “We glory in tribulations also, knowing that tribulation produces perseverance. Perseverance, character. And character, hope.” The trouble is not random. It is raw material.

C. S. Lewis once wrote, “Hardships often prepare ordinary people for an extraordinary destiny.” And one of those old school coaches used to tell his players, “Everybody wants to win on Saturday. The question is, who chose the right pain on Monday.”

So here we are. Monday. The day of choosing.

Listen, Chris.

Most people treat Monday like weather. Something that just rolls over them. Too hot, too cold, too busy, too much. They brace for impact and hope to survive. But you are not “most people.” You are building something. Which means you cannot afford to let the week choose your problems for you.

That line from Jordan Peterson is sharp for a reason. “How much trouble would you want there not to be?” Hidden inside that question is a truth we try to dodge. You cannot have a meaningful life without trouble. The only real choice is what kind of trouble you are willing to carry.

You can have the trouble of a hard conversation at work, or the trouble of resentment that rots a team. You can have the trouble of disciplined focus on one deep task, or the trouble of shallow distraction and a pile of half finished nonsense. You can have the trouble of getting under the bar and feeling your muscles burn, or the trouble of a body that slowly falls apart because you never asked anything of it.

There is no trouble free option. There is only “this trouble” or “that trouble.”

And here is where calm thinking matters. When the pressure is on, your mind wants to grab the nearest, easiest thing. Anything that gives you a quick sense of progress. Clear a few emails. Scroll. Hide in “urgent” but meaningless tasks. That is how we end up drowning in avoidable chaos. We dodge the harder, better pain, and in its place we get messes we created ourselves.

You know that pattern. Put off the tough decision with a client, now you have three fires instead of one. Avoid the awkward talk at home, now the distance is bigger and colder. Skip planning your week, now every day is an emergency.

The way out is not more speed. It is more clarity.

This is where you shift from “goals” to “design.” From “I hope this week goes well” to “I am going to choose my trouble on purpose.”

So, picture your week like a system you are building. Not a wish list. A structure.

Ask yourself, quietly, right now: “What are the three pieces of trouble that are worth suffering for this week?”

One demanding task at work. Not ten. One. The thing that, if you did it with full attention, would move the needle. Maybe it is finishing that proposal. Maybe it is mapping out a process that will save time later. Maybe it is finally sitting down to think, uninterrupted, about strategy instead of reacting all day.

Name it. That is trouble number one. You will have to carve time. You will have to say no to other things. People might not like that. But this is good trouble.

Then, one uncomfortable conversation. Again, not ten. Just one. Who do you need to talk to? A colleague you owe feedback. A friend you need to apologize to. Someone at home you need to be honest with. You know the name already. It is the one that just popped into your head and you wanted to push away.

That is trouble number two. You would rather not. But you are not aiming for comfort. You are aiming for clean. For aligned. For truthful.

And then, one physical or spiritual challenge. Something that costs you, but pays you back. Maybe it is committing to a certain number of workouts, even if they are short. Maybe it is a walk in the rain with no phone, just you and God, letting the noise drain out. Maybe it is setting the alarm twenty minutes earlier to sit with scripture, or to actually pray instead of just thinking about praying.

That is trouble number three. It will feel small in the moment. But that is how you train your soul. Tiny, chosen hardships that build a spine.

Here is the beautiful part. When you choose those three on purpose, a strange calm shows up. The week is still full. Emails still come. People still want things. But inside, you know what you are truly on the hook for. You stop trying to fix everything. You carry the weight that is yours, and you let the rest be noise.

And this is where leadership comes in.

Leadership is not the title on your email signature. It is how you carry your burdens in public. When you walk into Monday with your share of necessary trouble already chosen, people feel it. You are not frantic. You are not complaining. You are not spreading anxiety like a cold.

You are steady.

You say things like, “Here is what I am focused on this week.” You take responsibility for your part, without drama. You do not dump your stress on others. You do not brag about how busy you are. You just quietly shoulder the load you picked.

That steadiness gives everyone around you permission to stop whining and start building. They see that trouble is normal. That it can be faced, not avoided. That a grown man can hold weight without needing the whole room to comfort him.

And do not miss this, Chris. You are not doing this alone. God is not watching from the bleachers, arms crossed, grading your performance. He is the one who said, “My strength is made perfect in weakness.” Meaning, you bring your chosen trouble, and He brings the power to carry it.

So, as you step into this Monday, ask yourself that question again, slowly.

“How much trouble would you want there not to be?”

Then answer it with your life.

Pick your three pieces of trouble for the week. Write them down. Pray over them. Commit. And when the lesser problems come knocking, you can say, “No thanks. I have already chosen my hard.”

Now, go get after it.
