Good morning, Chris.

Clear sky over you this Friday. Right now it is thirty seven degrees, it will dip just a touch to about thirty six before sunrise, then climb all the way up to a sunny sixty four this afternoon. No rain in sight, zero percent chance, just a crisp start and a clean, bright finish.

Out in the world, Papa John’s is back in the headlines, with a Qatari backed fund kicking the tires on a possible takeover. Markets in Asia are wobbling again, stocks down while oil climbs after an attack on tankers in Iraqi waters, and everyone is watching what that means for energy. And closer to home, gas prices are ticking up with the tension in the Middle East, and car shoppers are suddenly searching electric again, looking for relief at the pump.

But put all that aside for a second.  

Stop.  

Breathe.

Let the shoulders drop. Unclench the jaw. Just be here, in this moment, before the day starts asking you who you are.

“Be still, and know that I am God.” That is Psalm forty six. Not “do more,” not “prove more.” Be still. Know.

Paul told the Colossians, “Your life is hidden with Christ in God.” In other words, your real life, your real name, is not sitting in some spreadsheet or performance dashboard.

C. S. Lewis once wrote, “There are no ordinary people. You have never talked to a mere mortal.” That includes you, in the mirror, half awake, coffee in hand.

And I think of a coach in the locker room saying, “If you do not define what this team is, the crowd will. And they will be wrong.” Same for a man’s life.

So, Chris, this morning, before the world starts talking at you… let God speak first.

The world will ask you who you are. That is not a maybe. That is a guarantee.

Today it will ask with a smile. With calendar invites. With “quick questions.” With metrics. With likes. With silence.

At work, it sounds like, “So what do you do?” That is really, “Why should I value you?” It will try to define you by your title, your income, your latest deal, your last review.

If the week went well, the world will tell you, “You are winning. You are important. You are your success.” If the week was rough, it will whisper, “You are behind. You are your mistakes. You are what you failed to ship.”

By Friday, we start tallying. Wins, losses, check marks, missed boxes. You look at the inbox, the calendar, the bank account, the scale, the mirror. And if you are not careful, you let those things answer the question, “Who am I?”

Here is the thing. None of that is neutral. Every system around you has an opinion about who you should be.

Your company has a version of you it prefers. Productive. Available. Always on.

Social media has a version of you it prefers. Interesting. Hot take ready. Never boring.

Even your own fears have a version of you they prefer. Safe. Small. Predictable.

If you do not answer the question yourself, all of those forces will gladly answer for you. They will write the script. And you will wake up one day realizing you have been playing a role you never auditioned for.

So the real work, the quiet work, is deciding who you are and what you are building toward. On purpose.

Not who you are according to your job description. Not who you are according to your worst memory. Not who you are according to your last argument or your last raise.

Who are you before the emails, before the meetings, before the numbers?

You know this instinctively. When you slow down, there is a picture of the man you are meant to be. It is not about perfection. It is about direction.

A man who is faithful. Who keeps his word. Who tells the truth even when it costs him. Who leads with calm, not panic. Who uses his gifts to build, not just to win.

That picture is not sentimental. It is strategic. Because if you know that man, you can start making decisions that move you toward him, one small step at a time.

This is where leadership comes in. People do not follow someone who has it all together. They follow someone who knows what he stands for, and is willing to live it out in real time, flaws and all.

You can be brilliant, but if you are undefined, you are hard to trust. You can be competent, but if you bend to every pressure, people feel it. They may use your skills, but they will not rest in your leadership.

When a man knows who he is, he becomes a fixed point in a spinning room. The storm can swirl, markets can swing, headlines can shout. He may adjust his tactics, but his core does not flip every time the wind changes.

And that starts inside. It starts with you answering the question before the world does.

So let us make this practical, right now, this Friday.

At some point today, maybe over your first cup of coffee or in the car before you walk into the building, I want you to write a single sentence.

Not a paragraph. Not a manifesto. One sentence.

“The man I am becoming, five years from now, is…” and then finish it.

Do not write what sounds impressive. Do not write what your boss would want to read. Do not write what would get the most likes. Write the truest sentence you can about the man you are aiming to be.

Maybe it is something like, “The man I am becoming, five years from now, is a calm, disciplined, God honoring leader who tells the truth, keeps his word, and builds people, not just projects.”

Or, “The man I am becoming is a present husband and father, excellent at his craft, generous with his resources, and unshaken by circumstances.”

Whatever it is for you, make it clear. Make it specific enough that you can feel when you are aligned with it, and when you are not.

Then, choose one action today that lines up with that sentence more than it lines up with other people’s expectations.

If that future man is honest, maybe today you tell the uncomfortable truth in a meeting, kindly, instead of nodding along.

If that future man is present at home, maybe today you leave the phone in another room for the first thirty minutes after you walk through the door.

If that future man is disciplined, maybe today you keep the small promise to yourself about your body. The walk. The workout. The food choice.

One action. Not ten. Not a life overhaul. Just one move that says, “This is who I am, and this is where I am going,” even if nobody else notices.

Because this is how identity is built in the real world. Not by a single dramatic moment, but by hundreds of quiet choices where you choose your compass over the crowd.

And here is the deeper layer. When you answer the question of who you are with God at the center, you are not just inventing a brand. You are agreeing with what has already been spoken over you.

Beloved. Called. Chosen. A steward, not an owner. A servant, not a tyrant. A son, not an orphan.

You are not trying to earn a name. You are learning to live from the name you already have.

From that place, leadership becomes something different. You stop performing for people’s approval. You start serving from conviction.

You can say no without guilt, because you know what you are saying yes to.

You can absorb criticism without crumbling, because you know criticism is data, not identity.

You can celebrate others’ wins without envy, because their success does not threaten who you are.

The world will still ask you who you are. The difference is, now you have an answer ready. A quiet, steady, “Here is who I am. Here is who I am becoming. And my choices today will reflect that, whether anyone claps or not.”

So as this Friday unfolds, as the week’s scoreboard tries to tell you your worth, remember… you are more than your wins, and you are more than your losses.

Let the numbers inform you. Let God define you.

Write the sentence. Take the one aligned action. Lead from that place.

Now, go get after it.

“The world will ask you who you are. If you do not know, it will tell you.” — Carl Jung.
