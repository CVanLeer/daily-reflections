Good morning, Chris.

Thirty point eight degrees right now under a clear sky. Cold and still. You are heading toward a high around forty two point four today, low just about where you are now, thirty point seven. Zero percent chance of rain, so it is one of those crisp, bright days where the air bites a little but the sky is wide open.

In the headlines this morning… there is a column out of the Washington Post about a mom whose cancer has come back, and her refusal to face what might be the end of her life is tearing up her family. Overseas, Donald Trump is talking about delaying a summit with Xi Jinping unless China helps secure the Strait of Hormuz, with that conflict in the Middle East still shaking up oil and big power politics. And reports say the Israeli military hit an aircraft used by Iran’s top leader and other senior officials overnight.

A lot going on out there. Heavy stuff, big forces moving.

But put all that aside for a second.

Stop.

Breathe.

Right here, before the emails, before the headlines grab your attention again… just be still.

“Trust in the Lord with all your heart, and do not lean on your own understanding. In all your ways acknowledge Him, and He will make your paths straight.”

Jesus said, “Whoever wants to save his life will lose it, but whoever loses his life for my sake will find it.”

C. S. Lewis once wrote that God is not safe, but He is good.

And there is that old coach’s line… “You cannot win the game from the sidelines.”

Let those sit with you for a moment. Trust. Surrender. Stepping onto the field.

Now, let us talk about that ship.

“If the highest aim of a captain were to preserve his ship, he would leave it in port.”

You can almost see it, right. This beautiful vessel. Hull polished. Sails folded neatly. Ropes coiled just so. Perfectly maintained. Never a scratch, never a storm, never a risk.

And absolutely useless.

A ship that never leaves the harbor is technically safe… and totally failing its purpose.

That is the quiet temptation of a lot of lives. Keep it safe. Keep it predictable. Keep everything under control. Do not risk the awkward conversation. Do not risk the new role. Do not risk being misunderstood, or rejected, or stretched.

Protect your time. Protect your heart. Protect your reputation.

And one day you wake up and realize… you have protected yourself right out of the very life you were meant to live.

From God’s point of view, your life is not a museum piece. It is not meant to sit behind glass, admired for its lack of dents. You were built for mission. For movement. For seas that are not always calm.

That is the tension. You feel it. On one hand, you are wired to think ahead, to see the risks, to plan. That is a gift. On the other hand, that same gift can turn into a cage. You can talk yourself out of every launch, every voyage, because the forecast is not perfect yet.

Listen. You are not asked to guarantee the outcome. You are not the master of the sea. You are asked to be faithful enough to leave the dock when you are called.

There is a difference.

Faith does not mean you know how the storm will turn out. Faith means you know who called you to raise the anchor.

Think about the captain in that quote. A real captain loves his ship. He cares about it. He maintains it. But he understands something deeper. The ship exists for the voyage, not for its own preservation. And he exists for the crew, not for his own comfort.

That is leadership. You do not get the luxury of living just to keep yourself safe. There are people on board. There are people in your orbit whose courage is going to be borrowed from your courage, or whose fear is going to be amplified by your hesitation.

If you park your life in the harbor, you are not the only one who stays stuck.

Overprotecting becomes a subtle kind of disobedience.

Overprotect your time, and you never take on the responsibility that stretches you, that builds you into the man you are supposed to be.

Overprotect your heart, and you never let people close enough to actually love them, to be hurt, to forgive, to grow.

Overprotect your reputation, and you never stand clearly for what is true, never admit weakness, never say, “I am sorry,” or “I was wrong,” or “I need help.”

On the surface, it looks wise. Careful. Mature. Underneath, it can be fear dressed up as prudence.

There is a story that plays out in a lot of homes like that cancer headline you heard earlier. Someone refuses to face what is real. They cling to a version of safety. “If I do not say it out loud, if I do not look at it, maybe I can keep control.” And the people around them carry the weight of that denial.

In a different way, you can do that with your calling. “If I do not start, I cannot fail. If I do not commit, I cannot disappoint anyone. If I stay in port, I cannot sink.”

But here is the cost: you also cannot arrive anywhere.

From a faith lens, the question is not, “How do I make my life as safe as possible.” The question is, “What has God actually asked me to do, and what would obedience look like today, even if it is costly.”

Preservation is not the highest good. Faithfulness is.

And there is another piece. You, as a thinking, strategic guy, you love to optimize. You want the right system, the right plan, the right conditions. That is good. Just remember, plans are supposed to serve the mission, not replace it.

You can map the ocean all day. At some point, you have to untie the ropes.

So let us make this concrete.

Where are you circling in port right now.

Maybe it is a conversation you know you need to have. With a family member. With a colleague. With someone you lead. You have rehearsed it in your head a hundred times. You know it will be uncomfortable, maybe even messy. So you keep polishing the ship. “I will bring it up when the timing is better.” The timing never is.

Maybe it is a project. Something that has been on your heart. A business idea. A ministry. A book. A habit you know you are supposed to build. You keep waiting for more clarity, more certainty, more time. You redesign the plan instead of taking the first small, ugly step.

Maybe it is a step of obedience that is quieter, more personal. Turning away from something you know is dulling you. Or stepping into something that will require more of you. You hear that nudge, and you stall. “Later. When it is safer.”

What does “raising the anchor” look like in the next twenty four hours.

Not in theory. Not someday. Today.

It might be as simple as sending the text to set up the hard conversation.

It might be blocking one focused hour on that project and treating it like a non negotiable appointment.

It might be writing down, in one sentence, the thing you know God has been whispering to you, and then telling one trusted person, “This is what I am supposed to do. Ask me about it.”

The sea is never going to look perfectly calm from the dock. There will always be a reason to wait. To run one more scenario. To double check the ropes.

At some point, you have to decide what you worship more. Safety, or the One who calls you out onto the water.

You are not reckless. You are not called to ignore wisdom or throw people into storms they are not ready for. But you are called to move. To risk. To spend your life, not store it.

So as you step into this cold, clear Tuesday… picture that ship. Picture the harbor. Picture the open water.

And ask, “Where have I been preserving, when I am supposed to be sailing.”

Then, do one small thing today that proves your answer.

Now, go get after it.

“If the highest aim of a captain were to preserve his ship he would leave it in port.” — Thomas Aquinas.
