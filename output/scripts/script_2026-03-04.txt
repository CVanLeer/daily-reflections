Good morning, Chris.

It is an overcast Wednesday where you are, sitting right around fifty two and a half degrees to start. Clouds hanging in, no rain in the forecast, and you are heading toward a warm high of about seventy five. Tonight you will drift back down close to where you started, around fifty two again. In the world, tensions in the Middle East are spreading fast. In less than three days the conflict between Iran, Israel, and Iraq has rippled out and is now threatening hundreds of millions of civilians across more than a dozen nations. Markets are feeling it too. Stock futures are tumbling as traders watch this new phase of the U.S. and Iran confrontation. And if you need a different kind of sky, there is a total lunar eclipse, a blood moon, streaming online tonight. The last one you will see until New Year’s Eve of twenty twenty eight.

But put all that aside for a second.  

Stop.  

Breathe.

Let the shoulders drop. Unclench the jaw.  

You are here. It is Wednesday. You made it to this moment.

“Search me, O God, and know my heart. Test me and know my anxious thoughts. See if there is any offensive way in me, and lead me in the way everlasting.” That is the psalmist, inviting God to hold up a mirror.

Paul told the Corinthians, “Examine yourselves to see whether you are in the faith.” Not “examine everybody else.” Examine yourself.

C. S. Lewis said, “Humility is not thinking less of yourself, but thinking of yourself less.” Less defending. Less explaining. More truth.

And you know how a good coach talks before practice. “You can make excuses, or you can get better. You cannot do both.”

So this morning, just ask quietly, “Lord, show me what is mine to own today. And give me the courage to face it.”

All right. Let us talk.

It is the middle of the week. This is the day where it is very easy to feel like the whole world is in your way.

You are in traffic. Someone cuts you off. You are already behind. You walk into work and an email is waiting that blows up your plan for the morning. A coworker drops the ball and now it is your problem. At home, somebody forgot to do the one thing you asked them to do. By ten thirty in the morning, the story in your head is, “If people would just get their act together, I would be fine.”

On the surface, that story feels justified. You can point to evidence. “Look at what they did. Look at what they did not do.” But underneath, something quieter is happening.

Every time you make it about them, you quietly hand your power away.

Because if it is all their fault, then you are stuck. You cannot drive other people’s cars. You cannot rewrite their emails. You cannot live their lives for them. So you end up in this weird place where you feel righteous and powerless at the same time. Angry, but frozen.

There is a reason blame feels good in the moment. Psychologically, it protects the ego. It is like bubble wrap around your sense of self. “I am not the problem here. They are.” That little move gives you a hit of relief. You get to be the wounded hero of the story.

But that is the trap. That relief is like junk food. It tastes good right now, but it leaves you weaker.

Now, there is a second trap. When you start to wake up to your own patterns, you can swing hard in the other direction. You stop blaming others, and you start blaming yourself for everything.

You replay the conversation and think, “I am such an idiot.” You miss a deadline and it becomes, “I always screw this up.” You hurt someone and instead of owning it cleanly, you drown in shame. “What is wrong with me.”

Blaming others keeps you stuck in anger. Blaming yourself keeps you stuck in self hatred. Neither one is real maturity.

Real maturity is this strange middle ground. You tell the truth about your part, without beating yourself with it. You say, “Yes, they did what they did. But here is where I made it worse. Here is what I could have done differently. Here is what I can do differently by noon today.”

That is where the power is.

In the world you and I live in, there is a simple rhythm people have used for a long time to get free from the blame game. First, you admit the truth about the mess. Not the flattering version. The real one. “I snapped at my wife.” “I ghosted that email.” “I avoided the hard conversation.” “I was late because I did not manage my time, not because of traffic.”

Second, you make amends where it is needed and possible. You go to the person and say, “I was wrong there. No excuses. Here is how I want to do better.” Not a long speech. Not a performance. Just clean ownership.

Third, you release what is not yours. You stop re running the tape in your head, trying to control what people think of you, or what they will do next. You say, “I did what I can. The rest is in God’s hands.”

That three step move is how you break out of self pity. Because self pity loves to sit in the corner and tell the story over and over. “They hurt me. Life is unfair. No one understands.” Ownership stands up and says, “Yes, that happened. And here is what I am going to do with it.”

So let us make this very practical. Let us talk about by noon today.

Somewhere between now and lunchtime, you are going to feel that familiar surge of irritation. Someone is going to do something that triggers the line, “Are you kidding me.”

When that happens, just notice the first story your mind tells. Is it, “Of course. They always do this.” Or is it, “Here we go again. I cannot believe I messed this up.”

Then, gently, ask a different question. “What is my part here. What can I actually control in the next hour.”

Maybe your part is that you did not set clear expectations with that coworker. Maybe your part is that you did not leave the house early enough to account for traffic. Maybe your part is that you are running on too little sleep and snapping at people because of it. Maybe your part is that you have been avoiding a conversation and now the tension is spilling out sideways.

You do not have to take the blame for everything. You just have to claim the piece that is truly yours.

And then, do one small concrete thing about it before noon.

Send the clarifying email. Apologize for your tone, not theirs. Adjust your calendar so you are not always sprinting. Ask the hard question in the meeting instead of gossiping about how bad the meeting is.

This is where leadership comes in. Because the way you handle blame does not just shape your inner life. It shapes the culture around you.

If you are the person who always has a story about why it was somebody else’s fault, people learn to hide around you. They learn to defend, to deflect, to pass the problem along like a hot potato.

If you are the person who owns your mistakes out loud, something different happens. You walk into a room and say, “I dropped the ball on that. Here is how I am fixing it.” You do that a few times, and suddenly other people feel safe to be honest too. You create a space where the question is not “Whose fault is this,” but “What are we going to do about it.”

At home, it looks like saying to your family, “I was short with you this morning. That is on me. I am working on it.” You are not lecturing them. You are modeling something. You are teaching, without a speech, that real strength is not never being wrong. Real strength is being quick to admit when you are.

Owning your part is not weakness. It is command presence. It says, “I trust God enough to tell the truth about myself.”

There is a line that fits today perfectly.

“The man who blames others has a long way to go.  
The man who blames himself is halfway there.  
The man who blames no one has already arrived.”

That is the journey. From “It is all their fault,” to “I see my part,” to “I do not need to live in blame at all. I just need to live in truth.”

You are not going to reach that final stage by nine a.m. today. But you can take a step.

So here is the invitation for this Wednesday. By noon, pick one situation that is bothering you. One. Ask God, “Show me my part.” Own it. Do the next right thing about it. Then, let the rest go.

No drama. No long story. Just quiet, strong ownership.

You do that today, and the mirror will still be brutal, but it will not own you. You will be the one holding it, with God beside you, saying, “All right. Let us clean this up together.”

Now, go get after it.

“The man who blames others has a long way to go,  
The man who blames himself is halfway there,  
The man who blames no one has already arrived.”
