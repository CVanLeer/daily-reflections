Good morning, Chris.

It is a clear sky over you right now, about sixty degrees. Cool, clean, good breathing weather. You are heading toward a high around seventy four today, dropping back to about sixty tonight. There is a big chance of rain later, over ninety percent, so it may turn into one of those bright, then stormy, then quiet evenings.

Around the world, the mood is… tense. Markets are rattled as Mojtaba Khamenei is named Iran’s new supreme leader, and the United Kingdom’s chancellor is joining an emergency G seven meeting to talk about oil prices. Crude has already jumped past one hundred ten dollars a barrel, and stocks in Japan and South Korea took heavy hits overnight. On a lighter note, college basketball folks are already arguing about the next top twenty five, trying to guess where everyone lands after week eighteen.

But put all that aside for a second.  

Stop.  

Breathe.

Let the shoulders fall a little. Feel your feet on the floor, your hands on the wheel or the desk. You made it to this morning. That is not nothing.

“Give us this day our daily bread.” Not tomorrow’s. This day.  

Paul told the church in Corinth, “Now is the day of salvation.” Not when you finally get your act together. Now.  

C. S. Lewis said something that fits right into that: the present is the only place where time touches eternity.  

And you know how good coaches talk. “Win this play. Do your job. One snap at a time.”  

All of that is pointing to the same thing. God meets you in today. Not in the fantasy version of you. Not in the edited replay of yesterday. In this exact Tuesday.

So let us sit there for a minute.

You know how most drives to work go. The car is moving through real space, real light on the road, real people in other cars. But inside your head, you are not in the car at all.

You are replaying that conversation from yesterday. The thing you wish you had not said. Or the thing you wish you had said. You run it again and again, like maybe if you replay it enough, the past will finally obey you.

Or you are pre living the day. Imaginary arguments in the eleven o’clock meeting. Imaginary disappointments. Imaginary victories. You are rehearsing your lines for ten different futures that will almost certainly not happen that way.

And while all that is going on, the actual morning God handed you… slips by.  

The color of the sky. The sound of the turn signal. The warmth of the coffee. The fact that you woke up with breath in your lungs and a mind that works. It is like the movie of your life is playing, and you are in the lobby arguing with yourself.

Underneath all the theology and all the philosophy, there is a basic, stubborn fact. Life is never happening in any time but now. You never actually arrive in yesterday. You never actually arrive in tomorrow. When tomorrow gets here, it has a new name. It is called “today.”

So when we cling to the past, or try to live in the future, we are doing something very odd. We are saying “no” to the only reality we have been given, and trying to live in a place that does not exist.

That is why you feel that quiet friction inside when you get lost in regret. Or when you spin out in worry. Part of you knows, “I am not really here right now.” It is a refusal to inhabit the one room where God has actually promised to meet you.

And your brain, for all its brilliance, is not helping you with this. The brain is a prediction machine. It is always scanning for threats, always trying to run simulations. That is useful if you are facing a charging animal. It is less useful when the “threat” is a slide deck or a difficult conversation.

So the mind sells you this story:  

“If I worry about it enough, I will be ready.”  

“If I replay it enough, I will finally understand it.”  

“If I think through every angle, I will not be blindsided.”  

But here is what we know, and what you have experienced. Past a certain point, worrying does not prepare you. It just drains you. Rumination does not solve yesterday. It just re wounds you. Over planning does not guarantee success. It just keeps you from taking the one small action that would actually move things forward.

Anxiety overestimates the usefulness of thinking about the problem, and underestimates the power of doing the next right thing in front of you.

And grace… grace is stubborn. It refuses to be delivered to any time except now. You do not receive tomorrow’s strength today. You do not get yesterday’s peace back by replaying it. God gives you what you need in real time, as you step into the moment you are actually in.

That is why Lewis said, “The present is the only time in which any duty can be done or any grace received.” Duty and grace both live in the same address: this moment.

So what does that mean for a man like you, who thinks ahead, who strategizes, who cares about doing things well?

It does not mean you stop planning. It means you put planning back in its place. You plan with a clear head, then you come back to the only place you can act: today. You remember that all the strategy in the world is just potential energy until it becomes a conversation, a decision, a step.

It also means you stop trying to spiritually time travel. You do not need to fix your whole life this morning. You do not need to carry the weight of ten years from now. You just need to be faithful to the one square of ground under your feet.

So here is a small, concrete practice for you today.

Before a key meeting. Before you open the laptop. Before a hard call. Take thirty seconds. That is it.

Pause.  
Feet flat on the floor.  
One slow breath in through the nose. Hold. Slow breath out.

And then, quietly, “Lord, I am here. You are here. What is the one duty in front of me right now?”

Not ten things. One.

Maybe the duty is to listen, instead of rushing to prove you are right.  
Maybe it is to speak the hard truth, kindly, instead of avoiding it.  
Maybe it is to focus on this one task for twenty minutes, instead of scattering yourself across five windows and three worries.

You do that, and something shifts. You step out of the fog of imaginary time and into the sharpness of this moment. You stop trying to be everywhere and everywhen, and you become fully present where you are.

And in that presence, you will notice things. A nudge. A bit of clarity. A sense of “this is the next step.” That is grace arriving on schedule. Not early, not late. Right when you are actually here to receive it.

So as you move through this Tuesday, notice when your mind jumps to the past to scold you, or to the future to scare you. When you catch it, do not shame yourself. Just smile a little, like, “There you go again.” Then come back. Back to the breath. Back to the task. Back to the God who meets you in the present tense.

This is the only time you can love anyone. The only time you can obey. The only time you can change a decision. The only time you can enjoy the good gifts in front of you. The only time you can become the man you are called to be.

Not someday. Today. In this car. In this office. In this body. In this moment.

Now, go get after it.

“The present is the only time in which any duty can be done or any grace received.” — C. S. Lewis
