Good morning, Chris.

Right now it is about sixty two degrees, overcast, a soft gray kind of start. You are heading for a warm one though, high around seventy eight and a half, low near sixty two tonight. Only a three percent chance of rain, so it is pretty much a dry, mild Wednesday on deck.

Around the world, oil prices are on a roller coaster again, jumping toward one hundred twenty dollars a barrel, then dropping back as President Trump sends mixed signals on what is next with Iran and the big economies talk about tapping emergency supplies. On a different field, Team United States took down Mexico in the World Baseball Classic, while Shohei Ohtani got the day off from the lineup for Japan’s final pool game. And on the geopolitical front, Trump and Putin had their first call since the Iran war began, talking about possible ends to both the Iran and Ukraine conflicts.

But put all that aside for a second.  

Stop.  

Breathe.

Let your shoulders drop. Unclench your jaw. Let the morning get quiet for a moment.

“Search me, O God, and know my heart. Test me and know my thoughts. See if there is any offensive way in me, and lead me in the way everlasting.” That is the psalmist, asking not for flattery, but for truth.

Paul told the believers in Corinth, “Examine yourselves.” Not your neighbor. Yourself.

C. S. Lewis once wrote that we are “very anxious to change other people, but very reluctant to change ourselves.”

And there is that old coaching line, “You are not what you say you are. You are what you repeatedly do.”

So this morning, just ask, quietly, “Lord, show me what is true about me today. Not the story I like. The truth you see. And give me the courage to face it.”

Listen…

It is midweek. This is the day where little irritations stack up. People are late. Someone misses a detail. An email is sharp. A promise is half kept. And in your head you are going, “Come on, man. Get it together.”

You notice every dropped ball around you. The missed deadline. The lazy comment. The lack of follow through.

But when it is you… when you are the one who is late, or distracted, or sharp… the story sounds different in your head.

“I meant to call him back.”  
“I was just tired.”  
“I had good reasons.”  
“I did not mean it like that.”

Same behavior on the outside. Totally different verdict on the inside.

There is a line that captures this perfectly.  

“When we judge others we look at their actions and are blind to their intentions, when we judge ourselves we look at our intentions and are blind to our actions.”

That is the double standard. For them: only what they did. For you: only what you meant.

Your mind is wired to do this. When you look at someone else, you only see the surface. The missed meeting. The tone. The outcome. So you fill in the blanks with a simple story. “He does not care.” “She is lazy.” “They are disrespectful.”

But inside your own skin, you have the director’s cut. You know the pressures. The reasons. The fears. The hopes. So you excuse the same behavior. “I care, I am just overwhelmed.” “I am not lazy, I just needed rest.” “I am not disrespectful, they are too sensitive.”

You are not doing this because you are evil. You are doing it because your brain wants to protect your image of yourself. It is easier to say, “They are the problem,” than to say, “I blew it.”

The trouble is, leadership does not care about your private intentions. Reality does not move based on what you meant to do. It moves based on what you actually did.

If you promised someone an answer yesterday and they still do not have it, their world is shaped by your silence, not by the good intentions in your heart. If you snapped at someone, their nervous system remembers the sting, not the fact that you were tired and hungry and stressed.

You can be full of love and still cause damage. You can have beautiful intentions and still leave people feeling unseen, unsupported, or hurt.

That is where real growth starts. When you stop grading yourself on what you meant, and start grading yourself on what actually happened.

This is the kind of honesty people in the trenches of change have to practice. The kind where you look in the mirror and say, “Whatever my reasons, I did that. Those were my actions. That is what they experienced. I own it.”

No more, “Yeah, but you have to understand…”  
No more, “I did that, but you do not know what I was going through…”  

Just, “I did that. That was on me. I am sorry. Here is what I am going to do differently.”

That is not self hatred. That is strength. That is leadership. Because the moment you own your actions, you get your power back. If your behavior is just a reaction to circumstances, you are a victim of the day. If your behavior is a choice, you can choose better next time.

So how do you close that gap between how you judge yourself and how you judge others?

First, flip the lens.

With other people, you naturally see actions and assume motives. “He was short with me, he must not respect me.” “She missed the call, she must not care.”  

What if, just for today, you assume you do not know their motives? You see the action, but you give their intention the benefit of the doubt. “He was short with me… maybe he is carrying something heavy.” “She missed the call… maybe she is overwhelmed.”

You do not have to lie to yourself and pretend the action was okay. You just allow for the possibility that their inner world is more complex than the story you are telling.

Second, flip the lens on yourself in the opposite direction.

With you, you naturally see your motives and blur your actions. “I meant well, so it is fine.”  

What if, just for today, you assume people experience you only through what you actually do and say. Not what you meant. Not what you planned. What you did.

You can still hold on to your intentions. They matter to God. He sees the heart. But people see your footsteps, not your heartbeats. So you ask, “If someone only had my actions to go on, what would they conclude about me?”

That question is humbling. And that is good. Humility is the doorway to cleaner relationships.

Here is a simple reset for this Wednesday.

Pick one relationship. One interaction that you know is a little tense. Maybe someone at work who has been annoying you. Maybe a family member. Maybe that one person you keep replaying conversations with in your head.

Today, with that person, do two things.

Number one. Assume their intentions are better than their recent actions. You do not have to trust blindly. You just hold the possibility that they are not out to get you. That they are clumsy, not cruel. Human, not heartless. Let that soften your tone and your face.

Number two. Hold yourself to the standard of your visible actions, not your internal story. If you say you will call, call. If you know you were short yesterday, do not explain it away. Own it. “Hey, I was off yesterday. That is on me. I am sorry.” If you promised help, show up with help, not just excuses.

You do not have to fix every relationship this week. Just pick one interaction and live this way on purpose.

Assume more grace for them. Demand more honesty from yourself.

When you do that, something shifts. You move from judge to servant. From critic to builder. From “Why can they not get it together?” to “What can I own here?”

And in that space, God can actually work. Because you are not hiding behind your good intentions anymore. You are bringing your real actions into the light, and asking for strength to do better.

So as you step into this day, pay attention to the moments where you feel that flash of irritation. That urge to write someone off. That quiet voice that says, “They always…” or “They never…”

Pause. Breathe. Ask, “What do I not know about their story?” Then turn the light back on yourself and ask, “What did I actually do here? What am I actually doing right now?”

You are not called to be perfect, Chris. You are called to be honest. To be teachable. To be willing to let God align your actions with the intentions he has planted in your heart.

One conversation. One interaction. One honest apology if needed. One follow through where you usually would have an excuse.

That is how you change a week. One concrete action at a time.

Now, go get after it.
