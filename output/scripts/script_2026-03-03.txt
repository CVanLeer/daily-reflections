Good morning, Chris.

It is an overcast Tuesday out there. Sitting right around forty seven degrees this morning. You are looking at a high of about sixty one, low right where you started, and just a tiny chance of rain, about one percent. So, gray skies, but dry and workable.

Out in the world, Kimora Lee Simmons is making headlines, looking to officially drop a couple of ex last names, including Russell Simmons. On the heavier side, markets are rattled. Dow futures are down big, over six hundred points, after a United States strike on Iran sent oil prices jumping and raised fears that this war could widen. And in tech, some of the folks who won big in the smartphone era are betting that the next wave of gadgets will not even look like gadgets. No screens, blended into everyday life, and maybe recording you without you realizing it.

But put all that aside for a second.  
Stop.  
Breathe.

Let the noise dim for a moment.

“Search me, O God, and know my heart. Test me and know my anxious thoughts. See if there is any offensive way in me, and lead me in the way everlasting.”

Remember that. The prayer is not, “Make this easy.” It is, “Show me what is real in me, and lead me.”

C. S. Lewis said that God whispers in our pleasures, speaks in our conscience, but shouts in our pains. Pain as a megaphone.

And you know how coaches talk. The good ones. “I am not here to make you comfortable. I am here to make you better.”

So as you sit here, early, before the day really starts, open your hands a little.  
“Lord, do not just make this day smooth. Make it true. Even if true is not comfortable.”

Let us talk about that.

Listen to how most of our quick prayers sound.  
“God, help this meeting go well.”  
“Please let them say yes.”  
“Take this stress away.”  
“Just let me get through this week.”

Underneath all of that is a pretty simple request.  
Make this easier.  
Make this smoother.  
Make this stop hurting.

Almost never do we naturally say, “Show me what is true here, even if it stings. Even if it complicates my life. Even if it makes me change.”

But that is actually closer to what scripture models.

You look at the stories. Joseph did not get the “make this easier” version of his life. He got betrayal, slavery, prison, delay. Years of it. David did not get the “skip the wilderness” package. He got caves, enemies, confusion. Paul did not get the “no thorns, no shipwrecks” plan. He got beatings, rejection, and something in his flesh that God refused to remove.

And when they cried out, God did not always answer with relief on demand. He answered with presence. With direction. With a deeper kind of strength.

“My grace is sufficient for you. My power is made perfect in weakness.”

That is not the comfort we usually want. We want the pain gone. God seems far more interested in the person we are becoming inside the pain.

If you zoom out, you can see a pattern. Discomfort, delay, and what feel like unanswered prayers are not evidence that God forgot you. They are often the tools He uses to grow roots. To deepen you. To pull out illusions.

But your mind does not like that. Your brain is wired for short term comfort. It wants the sugar rush, not the slow nutrition.

There is a part of you that will always pick the warm lie over the cold truth, at least at first. That part hates tension. It hates the feeling that something does not add up. So it does little tricks.

You know these tricks.

You say, “My job is not that bad,” even though you feel dead by Sunday night. Because if you admitted the truth, you would have to face fear, risk, change.

You say, “I can stop this habit any time,” even though you have tried, and you know you are not really in control. Because if you admitted the truth, you would have to ask for help, and your pride does not like that.

You say, “The relationship is fine, we are just stressed,” even though the same wounds keep reopening. Because if you admitted the truth, you might have to have the hard conversation. Or even walk away.

That is your brain avoiding pain, not seeking truth.

It would rather keep you in a familiar, low grade misery than push you through the sharp, short pain of seeing clearly and changing.

And here is where faith and psychology collide.

On one hand, you have a God who loves you too much to leave your illusions intact. He loves you enough to say no. To let the discomfort stay. To let your strategies fail. So that you finally say, “Okay. Show me what is true. I am done pretending.”

On the other hand, you have a mind that will fight that. It will rationalize. It will blame. It will distract. It will reach for anything that numbs, just to avoid the sharp edge of reality.

So you end up in this tug of war.  
God pulling you toward truth that heals.  
Your brain pulling you toward comfort that slowly hollows you out.

And this is where that line from C. S. Lewis hits so hard. He said, “If you look for truth, you may find comfort in the end. If you look for comfort, you will not get either comfort or truth. Only soft soap and wishful thinking to begin, and in the end, despair.”

That is the trap.

If you make comfort the goal, you get a little soap, a little daydream, and then the quiet despair of a life that never really changed.

If you make truth the goal, you walk through some fire. You feel the sting of seeing yourself clearly. You feel the disruption of admitting, “This is not working. This is not healthy. This is not honest.” But on the other side, there is a comfort that is solid. Because it is built on reality, not on pretending.

So here is the question for you today, Chris.  
When you pray, when you plan, when you think about the thing that is weighing on you right now…  
Are you really asking God for truth, or are you asking Him for comfort?

It is not that asking for relief is wrong. Jesus Himself asked that the cup pass from Him. But He did not stop there. “Not my will, but Yours be done.” In other words, “I know what would be more comfortable. But I trust what is true, what is right, what You see.”

You and I rarely get that far. We stop at, “Take this away.”

So let us make it concrete.

Think of one area of your life that has some tension around it right now. You probably felt it as soon as I said that. A habit you know is not aligned with who you want to be. A relationship that is not quite honest. A work situation that is quietly eroding you. A pattern with money, or with your phone, or with your time.

Today, instead of just asking, “God, please make this easier,” I want you to pray a different prayer.

“Lord, show me what is true here. Show me what is really going on, in me and around me. Even if it stings. Even if it disrupts my comfort. I trust You more than I trust my desire to feel good.”

Then, when something comes into focus, do not run from it.

Maybe the truth is, “I am using this habit to avoid feeling lonely.”  
Maybe the truth is, “I stay late at work because I do not want to go home and face that conversation.”  
Maybe the truth is, “I talk about trusting You, but I am actually trusting my own control.”

Whatever it is, take one honest action today that lines up with that truth.

Make one phone call.  
Have one real conversation.  
Tell one person the actual story, not the polished version.  
Change one small behavior that you know is out of line.

Not ten things. Just one. One step that says, “I choose truth over comfort, because I believe real comfort lives on the far side of truth.”

That is how transformation happens. Not in some dramatic, overnight miracle. But in a thousand small moments where you choose to see clearly, and to act in line with what you see.

And remember, you are not doing this alone. The same God who exposes the wound is the God who heals it. The same Spirit who convicts you is the one who comforts you. The cross itself is the picture of this. Truth and love, pain and redemption, all in one place.

So as you head into a gray, cool Tuesday, let this be the quiet, stubborn commitment under everything else.

“Do not just make it easy, Lord. Make it true. And give me the courage to walk in that truth.”

Now, go get after it.

“If you look for truth, you may find comfort in the end; if you look for comfort you will not get either comfort or truth only soft soap and wishful thinking to begin, and in the end, despair.”  
— C. S. Lewis
