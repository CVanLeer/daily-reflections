Good morning, Chris.  

Clear skies over you this Sunday. Sitting around sixty six degrees right now, heading up to a mild high near seventy two, with a cool low around forty four tonight. Zero chance of rain, so it is one of those clean, blue-sky days that almost dares you to start fresh.  

Around the world, it is not so calm. Iran is hitting back after United States and Israeli strikes, and President Trump is talking about “major combat operations” and even regime change. Oil markets are on edge as those strikes raise questions about a big chunk of the world’s supply. And tomorrow the Supreme Court takes up a strange case, asking whether people who use marijuana can legally own guns, and it is creating some unusual alliances in Washington.  

But put all that aside for a second.  

Stop.  
Breathe.  

Let the shoulders drop. Let the jaw unclench. You are here. It is Sunday. You made it to this morning.  

“Though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death, I will fear no evil, for You are with me.” That is Psalm twenty three. Not “if” I walk through the valley. “Though.” When.  

Paul told the Romans, “We rejoice in our sufferings, because we know that suffering produces perseverance. Perseverance, character. And character, hope.”  

C. S. Lewis said, “God whispers to us in our pleasures, speaks in our conscience, but shouts in our pains. It is His megaphone to rouse a deaf world.”  

And you know how a good coach will say, “What you think is the end, is usually the beginning of who you are supposed to become.”  

Hold all of that for a moment. Let it sit in the room with you.  

Because today, I want to talk to you about this.  

Planted, not buried.  

You know that feeling of being buried.  

Buried under responsibilities.  
Buried under expectations.  
Buried under regret for what you did, or what you did not do.  
Buried under uncertainty, where every path in front of you looks foggy, and you are tired of pretending you see more than you actually do.  

Being buried feels like this.  
Dark.  
Tight.  
Lonely.  
No clear way out.  

And when you are there, the mind starts telling a story.  
“This is it.”  
“This is who I am now.”  
“I blew my chance.”  
“Nothing good can come out of this.”  

But here is the thing no seed ever complains about.  

From the inside, being planted and being buried feel almost the same.  
Same darkness.  
Same weight of the soil.  
Same silence.  
Same sense that the world has moved on above you.  

The difference is purpose.  

Buried is meant to stay down.  
Planted is meant to break through.  

Faith says this. What feels like a grave can be a garden in the hands of God. That same soil that feels heavy and suffocating can become the exact environment where roots go down and strength builds up.  

“Those who hope in the Lord will renew their strength. They will mount up with wings like eagles.” That is Isaiah. But before the wings, there is weight. Before the rising, there is the being pressed down.  

In the life of anyone who has gotten sober, or tried to heal their heart, or rebuild their life, the darkest seasons almost always become the turning points.  

The job loss that stripped away your false security.  
The relationship that blew up and forced you to look at your patterns.  
The night you woke up and realized, “I cannot keep living like this.”  
The relapse that shattered your pride and finally made you willing to ask for help, really ask.  

When you look back, those are the days that mark the beginning of the new life. But when you were in them, they felt like the end.  

And in that space, you are not asked to see the whole plan.  
You are not asked to map the ten year strategy.  
You are asked to do something much smaller.  

One day.  
This day.  

Can I stay honest today.  
Can I stay faithful today.  
Can I not pick up the old coping mechanism today.  
Can I make one phone call.  
Can I drink one glass of water instead of numbing out.  
Can I tell God the truth about where I am, even if I am angry or ashamed.  

That is how people rise from rock bottom. Not by having the blueprint, but by taking the next right step, one day at a time, until the pattern of their life quietly changes.  

There is also a shift that happens in the mind.  

At first the question is, “Why is this happening to me.”  
Why this loss.  
Why this delay.  
Why this weakness that just will not go away.  

And that is a human question. There is no shame in it. God can handle your “why.”  

But if you stay there, “why” turns into a cage. It loops. It rarely gives a satisfying answer.  

So at some point, the question has to evolve.  

“What might this be preparing me for.”  

That little change in wording is the difference between buried and planted.  

“Why is this happening to me” says, “I am the victim of this moment.”  
“What might this be preparing me for” says, “I am being shaped by this moment.”  

When you see your pain as information instead of identity, everything shifts.  

Information says,  
“I feel lonely. That means I am built for connection. Where can I reach out today.”  
“I feel ashamed. That means something in me knows I am meant for more. What is the smallest act of integrity I can take.”  
“I feel stuck. That means some old strategy has stopped working. What might need to change.”  

Identity says,  
“I am lonely. I am shame. I am stuck.”  

Information you can work with.  
Identity you just sit under.  

And the enemy of your soul would love for you to confuse the two. To take this season and stamp it on your forehead as your name.  

But God never calls you by your failure. He calls you by your future.  

So here is a simple, practical thing for this morning.  

Name one area of your life that feels like a grave right now.  

Maybe it is your finances.  
Maybe it is your health.  
Maybe it is your marriage, or your dating life.  
Maybe it is your spiritual life that feels dry and distant.  
Maybe it is your sense of purpose, like you are just going through the motions.  

Pick one. Just one.  

Now, for the next seven days, I want you to deliberately rename that place.  

Not a grave.  
A garden.  

You can even say it out loud, “This is not a grave. This is a garden.”  

And then, each day, choose one small, concrete action to water that seed. Not to fix the whole thing. Just to water it.  

If it is your health, maybe today you go for a ten minute walk.  
If it is your finances, maybe you open the bank app and actually look, without numbing, and you move ten dollars into savings.  
If it is your marriage, maybe you send a simple, kind message without expecting anything back.  
If it is your inner life, maybe you sit with God for five minutes and say, “I am here. I do not know what to pray. But I am here.”  

And then you leave the seed in the ground.  

You do not dig it up every few hours to see if it is growing. That is what we tend to do. We start a new habit, or a new boundary, or a new way of living, and then we rip it up with doubt.  

“Is this working.”  
“I do not see change yet.”  
“Maybe I should just go back to what I know.”  

Seeds do their best work in the dark, in silence, when nothing seems to be happening. Roots first, then shoots. Depth before height.  

Your job is not to force the growth. Your job is to show up with the watering can. Small, faithful, daily.  

And as you do, something else will happen. You will begin to see that even your past “burials” were actually plantings.  

You will look back at the seasons that almost killed you, and you will realize,  
“That breakup taught me how to love better.”  
“That job loss pushed me into the work I was actually made for.”  
“That season of anxiety taught me how to be gentle with other people who are struggling.”  
“That time I hit bottom is the reason I am still alive, still sober, still growing today.”  

None of that makes the pain pretty. Pain is not pretty. The cross was not pretty.  

But it does mean the pain was not pointless. And that is a very different thing.  

So as you step into this clear, blue Sunday, I want you to carry this in your chest.  

You are not buried.  
You are planted.  

The weight you feel is not the lid of a coffin. It is the pressure of the soil that will one day hold your roots.  

The darkness you feel is not the absence of God. It is the hidden place where He is working beneath the surface, quietly, patiently, waiting for the right time to bring you through.  

You do not need to see the whole garden today. Just water the seed that is in front of you. One choice. One conversation. One act of honesty. One act of courage.  

God can do a lot with that.  

Now, go get after it.
